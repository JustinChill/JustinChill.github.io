Alex Ovechkin's path to #1

How Alex Ovechkin broke Wayne Gretzky’s once-unbreakable record

A new goal standard

When Wayne Gretzky, “the Great One”, retired from hockey in 1999, many assumed that his goal record of 894 would last forever. But for the past few years, the countdown has been on for when Alexander Ovechkin, better known as Ovi, might eclipse him. On April 6th he did just that, ripping a wrist shot past right the New York Islanders’ goaltender. It was his 895th goal. As was only fitting, he had fired the shot from the spot on the ice that has come to be known as “Ovi’s office”—the area in and around the left faceoff circle.

Mr. Ovechkin’s prowess stands out in two dimensions. The first is his remarkable longevity. As shown in the chart above, a basic tracker of cumulative goals scored, Mr. Gretzky was far more effective at putting pucks in the net early in his career. Through age 29 he outperformed Ovi by a wide margin. In his 30s, though, Mr. Gretzky was scoring just a bit better than the league average. Other prolific goal scorers have experienced similar declines, with their production dropping off as they advance on the aging curve.

Not so Ovi. Even this season, at age 39, he missed five weeks for a fractured fibula and, upon returning to action, promptly resumed scoring at his prodigious pace. Back in 2006, in just his second year in the NHL, he explained to reporters after a potential injury that he was fine, saying “Russian machine never breaks”. That captures something about his playing style. Even as his skating has slowed, he still has a stunning ability to read the play, find openings and fire off exquisitely placed shots.

Ovechkin's scoring has remained remarkably consistent, while Gretzky faded with age

The other thing that stands out about Ovi’s scoring is the context. Mr. Gretzky’s record had been expected to last partly because of all the changes to the sport of ice hockey since his day. Bigger goaltenders, tougher checking and more suffocating defensive systems have yielded fewer scoring opportunities. But that has never deterred Ovi (see chart 2). Prior to age 30 he was on average about 3.3 standard deviations better than league average at scoring goals. Since then, from ages 30 to 39, he has exceeded the average by 2.8 standard deviations. Mr. Gretzky was on average three standard deviations better before age 30, and only half a standard deviation better after age 30.
