Type Units

The measurements you use every dayâ€” such as inches and feet or centimetersâ€” are fixed, or absolute, measurements. That is, an inch is always an inch and a meter is always a meter. Type has its own equivalents, which are the pica and its subdivision, the point. The height of type is measured in points, and the width of a letter or a line of type is measured in picas. Point size is the height of the body of a letter in a typeface; originating in metal type, it was a slug of lead the typeface was set upon. The width of a typeface is measured in characters per pica.

The fundamental relative unit in typography is the em. An em is the same size as the type currently being set, so if youâ€™re setting 11-point type, an em equals 11 points. Despite the sound of its name, an em is not the width of an M; in fact, an M is rarely a full em wide.

The ex unit is useful when you want to size something in relation to the height of your text's lowercase letters.
