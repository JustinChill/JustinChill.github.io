The Worm That Could Not Be Caged

How Stuxnet opened Pandora’s box

It was January 2010 when officials with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) first noticed something unusual happening at the uranium-enrichment plant outside Natanz, in central Iran. Inside the facility’s large centrifuge hall, buried more than fifty feet beneath the desert surface, thousands of centrifuges were spinning at supersonic speed. But over the previous weeks workers had been removing batches of them and replacing them with new ones — at a startling rate. Even under normal conditions Iran has to replace up to 10 percent of its roughly 8,700 centrifuges each year. As the inspectors added up the centrifuges removed over several weeks in December 2009 and early January, they realised that Iran was ploughing through them at an unusual rate. Iran was not required to say why it had replaced them, and the inspectors had no right to ask. The IAEA’s mandate was to monitor what happened to uranium, not to keep track of failed equipment. What the inspectors did not know was that the answer to their question was right beneath their noses, buried in the bits and memory of the computers in Natanz’s industrial control room. Six months earlier, someone had quietly unleashed a destructive digital warhead on those computers with a single goal — to sabotage Iran’s uranium enrichment program and prevent President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad from building a nuclear bomb. It was, by most accounts, the first cyber-weapon to cause physical destruction in the real world; in the words of Michael Hayden, a former director of both the CIA and the NSA, “Somebody crossed the Rubicon.” It was also, almost certainly, the work of the United States and Israel.

That much is broadly accepted, even if no government has acknowledged it. What is less settled is what Stuxnet meant. A surgical strike that bought the West precious time against an Iranian bomb? An intelligence operation whose value lay not in the sabotage it inflicted but in the secrets it flushed into the open? Or the moment, as one veteran of America’s covert establishment put it, with “the whiff of August 1945” — the first use of a new class of weapon that, once demonstrated, could not be un-invented. None of these readings is wholly wrong, and none is wholly sufficient. This is the story of all three.

I. The state of the world, and the road to Natanz

Iran’s nuclear program is older than the revolution that supervises it. Begun under the Shah and stepped up in the 1990s, it had long been a source of quiet anxiety in Washington and Tel Aviv. That anxiety hardened into alarm in 2002, when an exile group revealed the existence of a secret enrichment facility buried in the desert at Natanz. Much of the outside world concluded that Iran’s protestations of peaceful intent concealed a drive for the bomb; Iran has always insisted its aims are civilian.

By mid-decade the trajectory was steepening. Ahmadinejad took reporters on a tour of the Natanz plant and talked of installing upward of 50,000 centrifuges — an extraordinary claim for a country with a single, Russian-fuelled power reactor. Olli Heinonen, the IAEA’s chief inspector, recalls that the uranium he encountered at Natanz in 2006 was “a very special uranium,” of a sort seen only in states that have had nuclear weapons. The equipment had been bought on the black market from the network of A.Q. Khan, Pakistan’s nuclear godfather. The Iranians, having paused enrichment while they feared an American invasion, watched the United States bog down in Iraq and Afghanistan and concluded the threat had passed. They resumed enrichment, installing cascades of centrifuges at the underground hall at Natanz.

For the White House, the options were dire and few. Conventional means were clearly inadequate: the dispersal of plants, their locations within mountains and other difficult terrain, and the secrecy surrounding future facilities all precluded an easy military response along the lines of Israel’s 1981 strike on Osirak. Several times the Bush administration reviewed military options and concluded “they would only further inflame a region already at war, and would have uncertain results.” And Bush, having gone through the entire WMD fiasco in Iraq, “could not even come out in public and declare that the Iranians were building a nuclear weapon,” as David Sanger put it. Condoleezza Rice told him, with characteristic brevity, that he had invaded his last Muslim country. Hawks around Vice President Cheney pressed for a strike; Bush recoiled.

Israel, which regarded an Iranian bomb as an existential menace, was meanwhile signalling that it might strike on its own. American war-gamers judged the scenarios uniformly ugly: some in Washington assumed that if the Israelis sent their bombers, the purpose “wasn’t to destroy the Iranian nuclear system, but the purpose of the raid was to put us at war with Iran.” “It’s 1938, and Iran is Germany, and it’s racing to arm itself with atomic bombs,” Netanyahu warned. Something had to be found between bombing and acquiescence.

It had been taking shape for some years, in the form of a slow-burning sabotage campaign. The United States exploited Iran’s enforced reliance on black-market middlemen, who were “open to western persuasion to sell defective components.” The CIA had introduced faulty parts and designs into Iran’s systems — even tinkering with imported power supplies so they would blow up — and in 2000 used a Russian intermediary to feed Iran an erroneous blueprint for a nuclear weapon. Centrifuge sabotage has a grim mechanical logic: the machines spin at supersonic speed, so when one explodes, the shrapnel causes others to break down — what engineers call “fratricide.”

Yet the campaign had not made a decisive breakthrough. When the European enricher Urenco tested Libya-bound centrifuges the Americans had tampered with in 2004, the verdict was deflating: the devices had not been sabotaged enough — “they still worked.” Douglas Frantz, who chronicled the CIA’s secret war on nuclear trafficking, put it with some acidity: had the initial sabotage efforts worked on Iran’s program, “we wouldn’t have to ratchet up the danger by releasing something like Stuxnet.”

In 2006, a group of intelligence and military officials showed up in President Bush’s office. “Sir, we have an idea. It’s a big risk. It might not work, but here it is.” The proposal centred on leaping the “air gap” that physically severed Natanz from the internet, and seizing control of the Siemens programmable logic controllers (PLCs) that commanded the centrifuges. The first stage was a “beacon” — code that would map the controllers and send back to NSA headquarters “the equivalent of an electrical blueprint of the Natanz plant.” Expectations were modest: the goal, one participant said, was simply to “throw a little sand in the gears” and buy some time. Bush authorised the effort. The codename was Olympic Games.

The bug needed testing. Under enormous secrecy, the United States built replicas of Iran’s P-1 centrifuges — an aging design Iran had bought from Khan. Conveniently, the Americans already owned some: when Muammar Gaddafi gave up Libya’s nuclear program in 2003, his centrifuges were shipped to Oak Ridge, Tennessee, and the Olympic Games team borrowed them for “destructive testing.” At Dimona, in the Negev desert, Israel ran parallel tests on the same P-1s. The two countries’ intelligence communities rolled together into a joint operation: the CIA led the Natanz attack, Israel’s Unit 8200 wrote the means of delivery. Washington brought the Israelis “deeply involved in every aspect of the program,” in part to dissuade them from launching their own pre-emptive bombing.

The tests worked. The bug would lurk for days or weeks before sending instructions to speed the centrifuges up or slow them down so suddenly that their delicate parts “self-destructed.” One participant described sweeping up the broken rotor tubes, flying them to Washington, and dumping the shards on the Situation Room conference table. The President picked up a fragment. “He was convinced this might be worth it, and he said, ‘go ahead and try.’”

II. The worm, the iterations, and the allies who lost it

The earliest attacks, in 2008, were small, careful, and mystifying. When centrifuges began spinning out of control, the Iranians — intercepted by American intelligence — assumed “bad parts, or bad engineering, or just incompetence.” No two attacks were exactly alike. The worm hid in the plant for weeks recording normal operations; when it struck, “it sent signals to the Natanz control room indicating that everything downstairs was operating normally. ‘This may have been the most brilliant part of the code,’ one American official said.” The Iranians grew so distrustful of their own instruments that they “assigned people to sit in the plant and radio back what they saw,” and, when a few centrifuges failed, closed down whole 164-machine “stands” in search of sabotage — and, in the manner of regimes that cannot locate an external enemy, fired people.

Ahmadinejad had been planning grand things; now something was quietly eating them. Then, in June 2010, came the discovery.

The call reached a small antivirus firm called Virus-BlokAda, on a drab Soviet-era block in downtown Minsk. Sergey Ulasen, the thirty-one-year-old head of its antivirus division, was sifting through suspicious files from a customer’s machine in Iran that kept crashing and rebooting. He and his colleague Oleg Kupreev found code that used a zero-day exploit — attack code targeting a hole still unknown to the software maker — to propagate. Zero-day exploits are the hacking world’s most prized possession because they attack holes for which no patch yet exists, and they are rarely found in the wild. Yet here the attackers were using one for a virus that, as far as Ulasen could tell, had been found only on machines in Iran. Something didn’t add up. He shared the sample with the wider antivirus community, and a name was picked from strings in the code: Stuxnet.

By August, Microsoft reckoned more than 45,000 computers worldwide were affected, almost 60 percent of them in Iran. “It is rare to see an attack using one zero-day exploit,” noted Mikko Hypponen of F-Secure; “Stuxnet used not one, not two, but four.” Late that September, Tehran conceded that the worm had infected systems at its civilian light-water reactor at Bushehr, with Hamid Alipour of Iran’s Information Technology Company confirming that “the attack is still ongoing and new versions... are spreading.” The more interesting question was Natanz. Mark Fitzpatrick of the International Institute for Strategic Studies put it simply: “If that were the case, it could be very significant.”

There was “an emerging consensus” that the worm had indeed hit Natanz, responsible for the apparent failure in 2009 and 2010 of about 1,000 of the 8,000 centrifuges enriching uranium there. David Albright of the Institute for Science and International Security (ISIS) told the Financial Times that it was “hard to believe Germany was not involved” — pointing both to the manpower and motive of the US and Israel, and to the Siemens know-how required. With fine British understatement, William Lynn, the US deputy secretary of defense, told the FT: “Where you have difficulties with attribution, it’s hard to guarantee assured retaliation because you don’t know who to retaliate against.”

At Symantec, Eric Chien and Liam O’Murchu began the laborious work of “deep analysis,” picking apart every byte. They quickly concluded they had something unprecedented: a worm “20 times the size of the average piece of code but containing almost no bugs inside of it. And that’s extremely rare.” Carey Nachenberg of Symantec put the figure even higher, at “50 times as big as the typical computer worm.” Analysts estimated it would have taken “a team of 10 specialised programmers about six months of full-time work,” with detailed knowledge of the target industry — a signature, said David Emm of Kaspersky, “indicative of involvement by a government, because of the level of sophistication.” Joe Weiss, a US industrial-control safety expert, called it “the first directed cyber weapon, or one aimed at a specific real-world process.” Ralph Langner, a German IT consultant in Hamburg, put it more bluntly. “My jaw dropped,” he recalled. “It is, in effect, a cybermissile,” aimed “to destroy something or to blow something up.”

A look inside the code

Symantec’s W32.Stuxnet Dossier — its authors Nicolas Falliere, Liam O’Murchu and Eric Chien spent three months dissecting the code — confirmed roughly 100,000 infected hosts across more than 155 countries by late September 2010. But the worm’s purpose was exquisitely narrow: to reprogram Siemens programmable logic controllers (PLCs) by replacing a Windows library file, s7otbxdx.dll, with a malicious twin of its own. Once installed, that twin could “monitor PLC blocks being written to and read from the PLC, infect a PLC by inserting its own blocks and replacing or infecting existing blocks,” and — critically — “mask the fact that a PLC is infected”: the first known rootkit not merely for Windows, but for the industrial controllers themselves. The attack was unlocked only on machines running Siemens’ SIMATIC Step 7 software, and even then only when it found a specific configuration of PLC — a fingerprinting so precise that Symantec judged it “a targeted attack on five different organizations in Iran,” with all twelve thousand subsequent infections traceable to just ten initial USB-stick seeds.

Inside the controller, the worm lay quiet for about 27 days, recording normal operating data; then it seized the frequency-converter drives that set centrifuge rotor speed. The Dossier identifies two specific families of drive in the payload — Vacon NX drives made in Finland, and Fararo Paya drives made in Tehran — and shows that the attackers expected a normal operating frequency “between 807 Hz and 1210 Hz.” During the sabotage sequence they pushed the output to 1,410 Hz, then dropped it to 2 Hz, then snapped it back to 1,064 Hz — cycling the centrifuges between violent overspeed, near-stall, and violent overspeed again.

A second, unfinished payload — “sequence C” — targeted the S7-417 PLC family and expected “six groups of 164 peripherals,” a structure that maps onto six cascades of 164 centrifuges. While the operator watched, the PLC rootkit “played back” the recorded normal frequencies through the monitoring system — so the operators saw nothing wrong even as the controllers were destroying the centrifuges. This was the technical meaning of Sanger’s “most brilliant part of the code”: the room upstairs was lied to while the machinery broke.

A biblical clue — or a flagrant act of misdirection — was buried deep in the code: the word “Myrtus,” a name associated with Queen Esther, “one of the leading figures of biblical Jewish history,” which security researchers took as a possible signpost to Israel. The Symantec analysts noted drily that Esther — originally named Hadassah — “means ‘myrtle’ in Hebrew.” Siemens’s own spokesman insisted the company had left Iran nearly 30 years earlier and had “neither been involved in the reconstruction of Bushehr or any nuclear plant construction in Iran, nor delivered any software or control system”; it knew of only fifteen infections reaching actual control systems, mostly in Germany.

Jumping the air gap

The enrichment hall at Natanz is not connected to the internet. Stuxnet therefore had to be carried in, almost certainly by USB memory stick, by a human — perhaps an Iranian scientist or technician, perhaps via Russian engineers working at Bushehr into whose “computer supplies” infected sticks could have been smuggled, perhaps (Iran alleged) an IAEA inspector, a charge the agency flatly denied. As one architect of the plan told Sanger: “That was our holy grail... It turns out there is always an idiot around who doesn’t think much about the thumb drive in their hand.” Whoever wrote Stuxnet also had access to confidential design information: the worm was triggered by Natanz’s specific attributes — centrifuge speeds, the type and number of “frequency converters.” As Albright points out, “they had to know something about Natanz that the IAEA does not,” which implies “somebody had to have a spy in Natanz.”

There was a “kill date” in the code — an act of unacknowledged restraint. “You need to have a cutoff date in there, and you can only do this and you can only go that far.” That date fell a few days before Barack Obama’s inauguration, suggesting an operation timed to expire at the moment of a presidential handover. In their final meeting before the inauguration, Bush urged his successor to preserve two classified programmes — Olympic Games, and the drone campaign in Pakistan. Obama “took Bush’s advice,” and reauthorised Olympic Games in his first year in office, 2009. Briefers would unfold the “horse blanket,” a giant schematic of Iran’s nuclear production facilities, across the Situation Room table; “sometimes it was a strike riskier and bolder than what had been tried previously.”

The variants, and the rift they reveal

What the code detectives eventually discovered is that the Stuxnet which escaped was not the first Stuxnet. Buried in its configuration data was a version number — 1.1 — and rifling through their archives, the analysts found “a few earlier versions.” The Symantec Dossier, working from 3,280 unique samples gathered by February 2011, mapped three distinct variants by compile time — 22 June 2009, 1 March 2010, and 14 April 2010. The June 2009 variant lacked the Windows “LNK/PIF” shortcut zero-day (MS10-046) and the USB-drive rootkit that hid its files from a casual user; it spread only with human help. The March 2010 variant added both — and accounted for 69 percent of all infections Symantec observed. The April 2010 variant was “almost identical” to March’s, but a different seed within the same organisation “resulted in significantly different rates of spread.”

The differences between the variants are almost a diagram of the rift between the two sponsors. “What NSA did was always low-key and subtle,” one insider recalls. “The problem was that Unit 8200, the Israelis, kept pushing us to be more aggressive.” When an earlier version accidentally infected unintended targets in 2009 — in countries far beyond Iran — American ROC workers “switched almost full-time to silently cleaning up infected computers to prevent the worm’s discovery,” for fear researchers would diagnose who was behind it.

Then, in 2010, came the slip that let containment break. The two partners had been aiming at a specific part of the centrifuge plant — a critical area whose loss “they had concluded... would set the Iranians back considerably.” But a programming error “led it to spread to an engineer’s computer when it was hooked up to the centrifuges. When the engineer left Natanz and connected the computer to the Internet, the American- and Israeli-made bug failed to recognize that its environment had changed. It began replicating itself all around the world.” The Americans maintain the Israelis “took our code for the delivery system and changed it. Then, on their own, without our agreement, they just fucking launched it.” The pressure came from the top: Meir Dagan, the head of Mossad, was “under growing pressure from the prime minister, Benjamin Netanyahu, to produce results.” Inside America’s Remote Operations Center at Fort Meade — motto: “Your data is our data, your equipment is our equipment — anytime, any place, by any legal means” — there was fury. The Israelis “were in a hurry, they opened Pandora’s box.” A US source put it to the makers of Zero Days: “Our friends in Israel took a weapon that we jointly developed — in part to keep Israel from doing something crazy — and then used it on their own in a way that blew the cover of the operation and could’ve led to war.”

In the worm’s wake, the human face of the campaign darkened. Over late 2010, Iranian nuclear scientists began to die. On November 29 that year, Majid Shahriyari — one of Iran’s leading experts on nuclear chain reactions — was killed when a motorcyclist attached an explosive to his car door on Artesh Boulevard in Tehran; on the same morning his colleague Fereydoon Abbasi-Davani survived a near-identical attack. Eight months earlier Massoud Ali Mohammadi, a quantum physicist, had been killed by a booby-trapped motorcycle near his home. Some pointed to Mossad, or to the exiled People’s Mojahedin acting on its behalf; others floated the darker theory that the regime itself killed men it had come to suspect.

In the Situation Room, Leon Panetta, the CIA director, with General Cartwright and deputy CIA director Michael Morell, “had to go down into the Situation Room and face president Obama, vice president Biden and explain that this program was suddenly on the loose.” “Should we shut this thing down?” the President asked. Told it was unclear how much the Iranians knew and shown evidence that the code was still causing havoc, Obama ordered the attacks to proceed. “Within a week, another version of the bug brought down just under 1,000 centrifuges. Olympic Games was still on.” It was Biden who, in what one attendee calls “Biden-esque fashion,” exploded: “It’s got to be the Israelis. They went too far.” Richard Clarke, the former counter-terrorism czar, summarised the triad of broken promises: “You told me it wouldn’t get out of the network. It did. You told me the Iranians would never figure out it was the United States. They did. You told me it would have a huge effect on their nuclear program, and it didn’t.” Soon enough, Israeli intelligence confirmed that a copy had fallen into the hands of the Russian Federal Security Service (FSB), successor to the KGB — and into the hands of the very country the worm was meant to cripple.

Did it work?

The question is whether Stuxnet worked. The honest answer, perhaps inevitably for a covert operation of this kind, is that it worked tactically and not strategically. Hillary Clinton declared in January 2011 that “their programme, from our best estimate, has been slowed down,” and internal Obama administration estimates put the set-back at 18 months to two years; Israeli estimates quietly stretched from “a year to 18 months” to Moshe Yaalon’s “at least three years” and Dagan’s prediction that Iran would not build a bomb before 2015. Yet enrichment levels “steadily recovered,” helping Iran by 2012 accumulate enough fuel “for five or more weapons, with additional enrichment.” In September 2009 western intelligence had already revealed a second enrichment facility tunnelled into the mountains near Qom — “operating well away from Natanz and the prying eyes of UN nuclear inspectors.” “I think we should assume,” Gary Samore, Obama’s top adviser on Iran, said bluntly, “that in the future Iran is going to try to proceed to build secret facilities.” Ronen Bergman, the Israeli security analyst, delivers the sharpest verdict: Stuxnet was “a major success and a huge fiasco at the same time” — discovered, escaped, and now the Iranians were aware that someone was trying.

There was a dissenting view, expressed in the Pentagon’s own house journal within months of the attack. Iran’s lightly enriched uranium output actually rose during the period of infection, as Iranian engineers improved efficiency — though not as much as it would have, had the damage not been done. The 1,000 wrecked centrifuges were replaceable; Iran was estimated to have stockpiled enough material for between 12,000 and 15,000 IR-1s, of which 11,000 had been deployed and 2,000 had broken — through routine use or by Stuxnet. The 18-months-to-two-years set-back was a tactical success with an uncertain strategic payoff — bought against the backdrop of UN Security Council Resolution 1929 of June 2010, the toughest round of sanctions yet, which was steadily squeezing Iran’s ability to procure more uranium or more materials for its current-generation centrifuges. For anyone concerned that sanctions were not biting fast enough, the window to strike covertly had to be “before the new centrifuges were introduced en masse.”

There was another reading, less discussed but arguably more perceptive, which treated Stuxnet less as a weapon than as an instrument of intelligence. Whatever it did to the centrifuges, its real value, the MIT security adviser Michael Schrage argued at the time, lay in flushing Iran’s nuclear apparatus into the open. “Targeted acts of sabotage disrupt, but the real pay-off comes from identifying the human and technical links in the chain of command.” Observing which scientists, administrators and engineers Iran trusted enough to bring in to deal with the breach — and when — was worth as much to Mossad, the CIA, the IAEA and GCHQ as a WikiLeaks data dump. The mole, in Schrage’s phrase, had become the worm.

III. The weapon that won’t go back in the box

“This has the whiff of August 1945,” a senior American intelligence veteran told the New York Times reporter David Sanger. “Somebody just used a new weapon, and this weapon will not be put back into the box.” The first thing Stuxnet did, paradoxically, was make its target stronger. Attacking a nuclear facility, even via cyberspace, “is an act of war,” argues Emad Kiyaei, an Iranian analyst; Iran did not sit by. The country announced the formation of its own military cyberunit, and very quickly dispatched a diplomatic note in malware. First it struck Saudi Aramco, the world’s largest oil company, wiping every line of code from 30,000 computers; then it surged against American banks — Bank of America, PNC, Wells Fargo — in the most extensive cyber-attack on US financial institutions ever launched from the Middle East. When Iran hit the banks, the United States could have shut down their botnet, but the State Department got nervous because the servers were not actually in Iran — so, until there was a diplomatic solution, Obama let the private sector cope. “People sat around in the White House Situation Room,” an NSA source recalls, “and said, ‘The Iranians have sent us a message which is essentially: stop attacking us in cyberspace the way you did at Natanz with Stuxnet. We can do it, too.’”

Cyberwarfare fears had been mounting for years: Russia’s large-scale attacks on Estonia and Georgia had “severely disabled their communication networks for brief periods” in 2007 and 2008, and China’s assault on Google’s systems in late 2009 had rattled Washington. But Stuxnet, as General Keith Alexander, head of the Pentagon’s new Cyber Command, put it, could cause “tremendous damage”: it was a different order of threat. Critics said the government’s response was too little. “Logically, it’s our job to protect the nation’s private infrastructure,” said Richard Clarke. “The fact that the government has no policy — except to defend the government — won’t stand the light of day.” Major General Richard Webber of the US Air Force was blunter still: “Those systems are very much wide open.”

Nitro Zeus

Stuxnet was almost certainly only the public face of a far larger American program. The NSA source describes a parallel operation, code-named Nitro Zeus, on which the United States “spent hundreds of millions, maybe billions,” involving “hundreds of personnel over several years.” Run out of US Cyber Command and the ROC at Fort Meade, it was “likely the largest and most complex cyber war plan the U.S. has ever created.” In the event Israel attacked Iran and America was drawn in, the plan called for attacks on Iran’s command-and-control, its military air defenses, and its civilian systems — power, transport, communications, finance. Operatives had already gained access to all the relevant systems and checked back on a near-nightly basis that the access was still live, with implants reportedly in the hundreds of thousands. “In comparison,” the source concludes, “Stuxnet was a back-alley operation. NZ was the plan for a full-scale cyber war with no attribution.” Britain’s GCHQ, the Zero Days film reported, also contributed intelligence that formed part of the bedrock for both operations — though its lawyers, especially over Israel and Iran, fretted over whether the cooperation could be reconciled with UK law.

Not everyone inside the American machine was comfortable with what they had built. Within the State Department and parts of the NSA itself, the Zero Days film records concerns about “the legality and ethics of some of these operations, which risked disabling civilian as well as military infrastructure.” State was seen by sister agencies as “a wet blanket” for raising anxieties about violating the sovereignty of third-party nations’ cyberspace. One confidential source told Gibney that some planners had “no fucking clue” as to the consequences: “You take down part of a grid, you can accidentally take down electricity in the entire country.” American hackers had done something similar before, perhaps without meaning to. In November 2012 almost the entire Syrian internet suddenly went offline; Edward Snowden later told Wired that the outage had in fact been caused accidentally by NSA hackers attempting to tap the routers forming the country’s network backbone.

Not Hiroshima, but a special operation

If Hayden’s “whiff of August 1945” has dominated the debate, the strategists at the Pentagon’s own university pushed back within a year. Writing in Joint Force Quarterly, Lukas Milevski rejected both reigning analogies: Stuxnet was neither the digital equivalent of a “fire and forget” missile nor a portent of cyber-nuclear Armageddon, but the first special operation in cyberspace — closer to T.E. Lawrence’s raiders blowing up a Turkish train on the Hejaz railway than to the bomb over Hiroshima. Surprise, the best ally of special operations, is now missing: Symantec has updated its definitions; Microsoft and Siemens have patched the entry wounds. But malware has one decisive advantage over human raiders — it can be in many places at once, compressing a sequence of raids into a single massively parallel assault.

A doctrine that may never arrive

Yet the debate that the nuclear age had — the twenty years of vigorous public argument that produced a doctrine of mutual assured destruction, inspection regimes and arms-control treaties — has no cyber analogue, and may never have one. For nuclear weapons, “the Russians come and look at our silos. We go and look at their silos. Bad as things get between the two countries, those inspection regimes have held up. But working that out for cyber would be virtually impossible — where do you send your inspector? Inside the laptop?” The norm that has emerged instead, says one lawyer of cyber conflict, is chilling in its candour: “the norm in cyberspace is do whatever you can get away with. That’s not a good norm, but it’s the norm that we have.” Samore, Obama’s WMD czar from 2009 to 2013, agrees: “It’s much more difficult in the cyber area to construct an international regime based on treaty commitments and rules of the road and so forth.” The Obama White House did push — quietly and largely fruitlessly — for an agreement on “rules of the game” of the kind that limit nuclear proliferation; attempts foundered, with suspicions that Israel, a long-time American ally, was the author of Stuxnet doing little to revive them.

The greatest obstacle to a doctrine, oddly, is secrecy. When Sanger published his account of Olympic Games in the New York Times in June 2012, the Obama administration opened a criminal investigation, targeting General Cartwright, rather than discuss it. The United States, Sanger notes, “has never acknowledged conducting any offensive cyber attack anywhere in the world.” To say the word “Stuxnet” inside the agencies was, one participant recalls, “like saying ‘Voldemort’ in Harry Potter.” When the worm hit the media, “they polygraphed everyone in our office, including people who didn’t know shit. You know, they polyed the interns, for god’s sake.” No charges were ever filed — possibly, it is thought, for fear that a prosecution would reveal classified details. Hayden, the former director of both NSA and CIA, is blunter than most: “This stuff is hideously overclassified, and it gets in the way of a mature public discussion as to what it is we as a democracy want our nation to be doing up here in the cyber domain.”

The antidote, Clarke argues, is the one that slowly tamed the nuclear, biological and chemical nightmares of the last century: an arms-control effort that the cynics will pronounce impossible and that will then take two or three decades anyway. “I was told, when we were beginning, ‘it’s too hard... you’ll never get there.’ Well, it took 20, 30 years in some cases, but we have a biological weapons treaty that’s pretty damn good, a chemical weapons treaty that’s pretty damn good, three or four nuclear weapons treaties. Yes, it may be hard, and it may take 20 or 30 years, but it’ll never happen unless you get serious about it, and it’ll never happen unless you start it.”

For now, the world lives in the interregnum. Sanctions and diplomacy, in the form of the 2015 Iran nuclear deal, eventually did what sabotage could not — an outcome the NSA’s insiders greeted with private relief, even as they noted that “behind that deal was a lot of confidence in our cyber capability. We were everywhere inside Iran. Still are.” The cyber-war scenario that shuts a country’s power grid so thoroughly that “all the king’s men can’t turn the lights back on or filter the water for weeks” is no longer hypothetical. It is the plan. As Hayden warns: “If we go out and do something, most of the rest of the world now thinks that’s a new standard, and it’s something they now feel legitimated to do as well. But the rules of engagement, international norms, treaty standards, they don’t exist right now.” The Pentagon’s strategists would remind us that special operations rarely decide campaigns on their own, and that the first of them in cyberspace was, as Milevski concluded, “operating entirely in unknown territory. Now, the right lessons need to be learned.” Whether they are learned, and by whom, remains undisclosed. The proof of concept, however, is established: a weapon built in the shadows has stepped into the light, and will not be persuaded to step back.

  11. Lukas Milevski, “Stuxnet and Strategy: A Special Operation in Cyberspace?” Joint Force Quarterly, issue 63, 4th quarter 2011, pp. 64–69. Milevski, a doctoral candidate at the University of Reading under Professor Colin Gray and winner of the 2010 RUSI Trench Gascoigne Essay Competition, argues that Stuxnet is best understood not as the digital analogue of a “fire and forget” missile or a portent of cyber-nuclear war, but as the first special operation conducted in cyberspace. Drawing on James Kiras’s theory of special operations and strategy, and on Martin Libicki’s observation that “cyber attacks are self-depleting,” he reads Stuxnet as a tactical success but a strategic failure: 1,000 centrifuges destroyed out of 9,000 at Natanz, Iranian lightly-enriched-uranium output actually rising during the infection period, the destroyed stock replaceable from a remaining cushion of perhaps 1,000–4,000 IR-1s. He argues the strike only makes strategic sense in combination with UN Security Council Resolution 1929 (June 2010) — the fourth and toughest round of sanctions — which had by then thinned Iran’s procurement options thin enough that attrition of stock could compound the pressure, before the next-generation IR-2 and IR-4 centrifuges were introduced en masse. He closes on the missing ingredient for any repeat: surprise, “the best ally of special operations,” is now gone — and on the disquieting possibility that, unlike human raiders, a program can be in many places at once, pointing the way toward massively parallel cyberattacks in future. ?
