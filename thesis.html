In a world where improved cognitive performance is a clear advantage, whether for a top student or a stressed professional, it’s not surprising that the idea of popping a pill to enhance your brainpower has broad appeal. So called "smart pills" are herbal extracts offered in the form of capsules, tablets, and powders which purport to improve memory, creativity, attention, motivation, and alertness in healthy individuals. The market for such supplements has exploded, with an estimated market size valued at $7.65 billion in 2020 and projected to reach $19.73B by 2030.

What are nootropics, do they actually work, and how do you go about choosing the most effective and safe brand on the market? Let's look at the history of these brain supplements and find out.

Noo- what now?

The term “nootropic” was coined by psychologist and chemist Corneliu Giurgea in 1972, who defined precise standards for nootropics. According to Dr. Giurgea, the main features of the nootropic profile consist of:

  • Enhancement of learning acquisition. A nootropic should help improve working memory and the ability to learn.
  • Improved cognition under stress. A nootropic should still manage to support brain function under adverse conditions such as low oxygen at the tissue level.
  • Enhanced resistance to brain aggressions. A nootropic should protect the brain from chemical or physical toxicity.
  • Demonstrated biological activity. A nootropic should show actual bio-activity rather than a placebo effect.
  • Facilitation of transfer of information. A nootropic should help information flow better across the brain (facilitation of inter-hemispheric transfer of information).
  • Absence of usual pharmacological effects of neuro-psychotropic drugs. A nootropic should not be toxic to humans and not have any significant side effects.

In general, a substance can be called a nootropic if it grants the user more control over their neurochemistry and the resulting behavioral and experiential outcomes.

Nootropics can either be synthetic and produced in a laboratory (e.g. piracetam) or can occur naturally as herbal plants like Ginkgo biloba and Panax quinquefolius (American Ginseng). Natural nootropics aid in promoting brain function with a simultaneous improvement in brain health. One of the most commonly used and consumed nootropics is caffeine - but it is far from the most exciting option!

The Nootropic Stack

Stacking supplements is beneficial in several ways. First, it can help make your supplements more effective so that you get faster and more pronounced results. Many compounds will work great on their own but have even better effects when combined or ‘stacked’ with something else. It might be that ‘supplement A’ activates pathways in the body so that ‘supplement B’ is absorbed more fully or can work more quickly. Or they may work synergistically by improving two elements of your biology, such as aerobic and anaerobic power in the case of Beta-Alanine and creatine.
